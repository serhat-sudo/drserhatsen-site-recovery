**1.1**

**My skin didn't sulk — it simply grew up**

*Collagen production begins slowing at 25. Knowing this turns panic into knowledge.*

> *A twenty-eight-year-old patient looked in the mirror and then told me in the consulting room: "Doctor, it's as if my skin has sulked." It hasn't sulked. It has simply grown up.*

Most women who have passed through adolescence and reached their mid-twenties recognise that sentence. One morning you look in the mirror and your skin no longer behaves as it used to. It looks duller, tires more quickly, no longer carries make-up the way it once did. This sense of "sulking" is common and almost always coincides with the same age range: between 25 and 28.

The reason is not emotional — it is biological. From the age of twenty-five, the collagen that forms the structural scaffold of your skin begins to decrease by approximately one per cent per year. During the same period, hyaluronic acid volume falls and the rate of skin cell renewal slows. This is not a sign that you are ageing — it is a sign that your body is moving into a different phase. Just as your skin changed during puberty, it changes again after twenty-five. The difference: this time, no one tells you in advance.

**Hormones and skin — a silent partnership**

For a long time, skin health was understood purely in terms of external care. Yet the skin is one of the external mirrors of hormonal architecture. When oestrogen falls, the skin thins; when testosterone rises, sebum production increases; when cortisol becomes chronic, the skin becomes inflamed. What women describe as "skin sulking" in their late twenties is very often the signature of this silent partnership.

This knowledge changes what you do for your skin. The conversation needs to include not only the surface but also the underlying infrastructure. Sleep, stress, nutrition, menstrual regularity — all are readable on your skin.

**The four steps of an evening routine**

Premium magazine pages display long lists. In real life, four steps are sufficient.

Step one: cleansing. An oil cleanser followed by a gentle water-based cleanser. This double-cleanse method removes make-up, sunscreen, and the day's accumulated impurities. Tried and tested brands such as Cetaphil or La Roche-Posay are adequate for daily use; your skin will give you feedback.

Step two: serum. After twenty-five, the cornerstone of your routine is vitamin C in the morning and retinol in the evening. Retinol can cause irritation at first; begin with a low-dose formulation twice a week and increase gradually as the skin adapts. Niacinamide can be added as a supplement — it regulates pore appearance and skin tone.

Step three: moisture. A moisturiser containing hyaluronic acid, peptides, or ceramides. The formulation changes with the seasons — richer in winter, a light gel in summer.

Step four: SPF. Only in the morning, but every morning. Eighty per cent of skin ageing is sun-related. This is not an exaggeration; it is a fact repeatedly confirmed in dermatological literature. Every day you skip SPF, you accelerate collagen loss.

**The invisible enemies of skin**

Smoking is the single external factor that ages skin fastest. It disrupts collagen production, weakens capillary circulation, and increases oxidative stress. Half a pack a day in your twenties is enough to make a visible difference to your skin by the time you reach thirty.

Sleep deprivation closes the skin's nocturnal repair window. The skin undergoes its most intensive renewal during deep sleep stages between 11 pm and 3 am. Skin that misses this window arrives at morning looking dull; if this is repeated for three consecutive nights it becomes visible to the naked eye.

Alcohol dehydrates and destabilises blood sugar. A woman in her twenties who drinks alcohol three to four times a week will show markedly more skin changes by her late thirties than a woman of the same age who does not drink.

I say this without judgement. Life in your twenties is this — late nights, occasional drinking, occasional smoking. But knowing that these choices leave a mark on your skin allows you to make conscious decisions.

**Who has really sulked**

I will return to my patient's words. The skin has not sulked — it has changed. The real question is whether we read that change as a loss. A twenty-five-year-old woman's skin cannot be the same as a twenty-year-old's; expecting it to be is a kind of elegy for a youth that is no longer yours to keep.

The skin maturing is the sign that you yourself are maturing. You will come to know it better, you will learn to care for it more wisely, you will make peace with its age. This is not a loss — it is the first adult relationship.

> ***Doctor's note ---** Persistent acne, rapid hair changes, or weight fluctuations that begin after twenty-five sometimes stem not from a skin problem but from a hormonal imbalance — for example, polycystic ovary syndrome. If your skin is sending you the same message repeatedly, the gynaecologist's door is open alongside the dermatologist's.*

**1.2**

**Premenstrual syndrome — or just a bad day?**

*Recognising PMS is the first step towards making peace with your body.*

> *A friend of mine sends me the same message every month: "Today everything feels unbearably heavy." Three days later her period arrives. She doesn't see the pattern; I do. Most women are the same.*

Premenstrual syndrome is a cycle that the vast majority of women live through but very few recognise. Studies show that around eighty per cent of women of reproductive age experience physical or emotional changes on specific days of the month. A significant proportion of these women label these changes "a bad week", "a day when I was stressed", or "unexplained sadness". A woman who does not name it as PMS never learns to manage it.

PMS is not a new concept in science. It was first described in 1931 and has been studied for decades. Yet in clinical practice it is still framed as "women exaggerating things". That framing is both unfair and incorrect. PMS is the result of the effects of oestrogen and progesterone fluctuations on neurotransmitter systems — particularly serotonin and GABA. It is medically real and culturally invisible.

**Symptom map**

PMS symptoms cluster into three groups.

Physical: breast tenderness, abdominal bloating, headache, lower back or lumbar pain, acne flare-ups, increased appetite (especially carbohydrate cravings), sleep irregularity, fatigue. The most prevalent in this cluster are bloating and breast tenderness, occurring in 60–70% of women.

Psychological: irritability, easy anger, anxiety, low mood, tearfulness, difficulty concentrating, loss of motivation. This is the least recognised but most powerfully disruptive cluster, because these emotions appear "without reason" and the person questions herself: "Why do I feel like this?"

Relational: conflict with close ones, a tendency towards social withdrawal, disproportionate reactions to minor events, unexplained touchiness. This cluster also affects partners and can become a source of tension in relationships.

**The habit of tracking — befriending your body**

The most powerful way to recognise PMS is simple: tracking. For a few consecutive months, while keeping a menstrual calendar, note your mood and physical state briefly each day. After three months, when you look at the table, you will see the pattern.

Apps such as Flo and Clue make this easier; over time they teach you which days tend to be energetic, which days you pull inward, which days you are most creative. As your data accumulates, your predictive power grows. If you know your period is coming next week, you can plan that week accordingly: move the important presentation earlier, postpone the difficult conversation, reduce your load.

A word of caution about these apps: some share your data with third parties. Read the terms before using them. Prefer those that are most transparent about data privacy.

**Nutrition and movement — practical support**

There are proven ways to reduce PMS symptoms.

Magnesium deficiency is common in PMS. A daily supplement of 200–400 mg magnesium citrate (taken in the evening — it also supports sleep) reduces headaches, back pain, and sleep problems. Food sources: almonds, pumpkin seeds, dark leafy greens, dark chocolate.

Vitamin B6 is effective for psychological symptoms. At a daily dose of 50–100 mg it reduces the irritability and low-mood cluster of PMS. Its effect is enhanced when taken together with magnesium.

Omega-3 fatty acids (salmon, sardines, walnuts, flaxseed) calm the inflammatory response and reduce PMS symptoms such as breast tenderness. Two to three portions of oily fish per week or a tablespoon of flaxseed daily is sufficient.

Regular aerobic exercise — 30 minutes, three to four days a week — increases endorphin production and markedly reduces at least the psychological symptoms of PMS. Resistance training is also effective; walking alone is not enough.

Reducing caffeine and sugar intake during the week before the period alleviates anxiety and sleep problems. There is no need to cut them out entirely; conscious reduction works.

**PMS or PMDD?**

For most women PMS is manageable. However, in a small group — approximately three to five per cent — symptoms reach a severity that brings life to a halt. This condition is called Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD).

In PMDD, being unable to go to work, severely disrupting relationships, or experiencing thoughts of self-harm can occur. It is not a hormonal disorder per se but an abnormal response in the brain to hormonal fluctuations. There is treatment — SSRIs, hormonal regulation, cognitive-behavioural therapy. Recognising PMDD is critical to avoid unnecessary years of suffering.

If your premenstrual symptoms are stopping your life, creating serious relational or professional problems, or carrying thoughts of self-harm — this may not be PMS but PMDD. It requires the partnership of a gynaecologist and a psychiatrist; a single door is not enough.

**Befriending your body**

What does it mean to recognise PMS? It means beginning to speak the same language as your body. At twenty it is common to treat menstruation as an obstacle — "again, now?" By thirty, for most women, that irritation softens; the body wants different things at different times, and recognising those needs becomes part of life.

This transformation does not happen by itself. Tracking, listening, accepting — these are active choices. The body literacy gained in your twenties becomes the foundation for your thirties, forties, fifties, and all the way to perimenopause.

> ***Doctor's note ---** Have your PMS symptoms changed noticeably, intensified, or acquired new features in the last three months? Thyroid disorders, anaemia, and depression can sometimes present like PMS. If you notice change over three consecutive months, it makes sense to come in for a check.*

**1.3**

**The first gynaecology visit — it begins with a breath and ends with a question**

*The first examination is as symbolic as it is medical. The right preparation eases both.*

> *A twenty-two-year-old patient walks into the consulting room one day and, before closing the door, says: "This is my first appointment. What should I do?" Clothes, hygiene, the right to ask questions — she begins listing. I say, "First, breathe." "Then the rest."*

For many women, the first gynaecology visit is something postponed for years, finally undertaken with great courage, and — once it is over — leaves them feeling lighter. It doesn't have to be that way. The first examination — like your first visit to the dentist — is a natural part of getting to know your own body. What makes it a momentous event is usually culture, not medicine.

In this article I want to explain not only what you need to do, but what you should expect, and what you should carry in your mind when you walk through the consulting room door. Because the success of a first appointment depends less on your doctor's skill and more on coming with your own questions.

**When to have your first appointment**

There is a commonly wrong answer to this question: "Right after your first period." Wrong. There is no need for routine gynaecological examination for adolescent girls — bodily monitoring after the first period is part of general paediatric health.

The right time for the first adult gynaecology appointment: first sexual intercourse, first significant symptom (abnormal discharge, pain, menstrual irregularity), contraceptive need, or age twenty-one — whichever comes first. If none of these applies, the age of twenty-one is a sensible first timing.

The subject of virginity cannot pass unremarked here. In Turkey, for many years it was believed that "unmarried women cannot be examined." This is wrong. Gynaecological examination is both possible and necessary for unmarried women. Hymen assessment is an entirely separate procedure and is carried out only when specifically requested. During a standard examination it is not addressed unless needed; assessing discomfort, discharge, or menstrual irregularity does not require a pelvic examination.

**Preparation — a simple checklist**

A few days before:

Remember your last period date. If you don't know it, give an estimate — there is no shame in saying so. If you have a tracking app you can find it there. Write down the names of any medications or supplements you are taking and bring the list — especially if you are using hormonal contraception. Briefly summarise any previous complaints or treatments.

On the day of the appointment:

Wear comfortable, easy-to-remove clothing. A skirt or loose trousers are ideal. Do not douche — vaginal douching within 24 hours before the examination disturbs sampling. Sexual intercourse is not restricted but if it has occurred within 24 hours, it may affect samples; it is enough to mention this to your doctor.

The most important preparation: your list of questions. Whatever is on your mind, however silly it may seem, write it down and bring it. Everything you have thought "can I even ask this?" can be asked.

**What happens at the appointment**

A standard first appointment consists of the following steps.

Consultation. The doctor first sits with you and talks. Complaints, history, menstruation, relationship status, family medical history. This part is the most important portion of the appointment — it provides more information than the physical examination. Take your time; don't hesitate to ask questions.

General examination. Blood pressure, pulse, and if necessary an abdominal examination. This part is not gynaecology-specific.

Pelvic examination. For a sexually active woman; if necessary and with her consent. It has two stages: speculum examination (visual assessment of the vagina and cervix) and bimanual examination (internal–external examination performed with both hands, assessing the uterus and ovaries). Together they take 3–5 minutes.

Additional tests if required: smear sample, HPV test, vaginal culture, ultrasound. These are not performed at every appointment; they are planned according to need. A smear is generally not needed before the age of 21; HPV testing becomes standard after the age of 30.

The pelvic examination may be physically uncomfortable but it should not be painful. If there is pain, don't hesitate to say so; a change in position or a different speculum size can be used. The question "does it hurt?" is not asking what is expected — it is asking about your actual experience. Answer accordingly.

**The right to ask questions — what is expected in premium clinical care**

The hallmark of a good appointment is not how much the doctor speaks, but how freely you are able to ask questions. In premium clinical care, the appointment does not end with "any questions?" — it ends with the doctor having made time for your questions.

Examples of questions you may ask: "What does this symptom mean?", "How often should this result be followed up?", "Is this contraceptive appropriate for me?", "Under what circumstances should I come back?", "Is there a reliable source you could recommend for me to read on this topic?"

If you do not receive an answer — or if the answer does not satisfy you — you have the right to seek a second opinion. This is not an insult to the doctor; it is normal in healthcare. In a premium medical environment, physicians expect this.

**Choosing a doctor — who, and on what basis**

If you don't get on with your first doctor, that is not a judgment about medicine — it is a question of fit. Some women want a gentler manner, others prefer more direct information. Some prefer a female doctor; for others it makes no difference. When you find the doctor you fit with, maintaining that relationship for years is valuable both clinically and psychologically.

Markers to look for when choosing a doctor: not being in a hurry in the consulting room, not dismissing your questions, explaining again when you say you haven't understood, respecting your privacy, a well-organised appointment system, and a clean clinical environment. These come after clinical competence but are the foundation of a lasting relationship.

> ***Doctor's note ---** The success of your first appointment depends not on your choice of doctor but on your courage to ask questions. Courage comes with time — a question not asked at the first appointment can be asked at the second. That is why the door is always open.*

**1.4**

**Sport and menstruation — let your cycle work for you**

*Reading your body means training with it, not against it.*

> *A twenty-eight-year-old patient was running five days a week — long distances, some races. One day it was her turn: "Doctor, my period has been irregular for the last six months." The first thing I asked was: "How is your training load?" The connection is usually in plain sight — it just needs to be looked at.*

Sport is one of the most powerful supports for women's health. It supports hormonal balance, lifts mood, protects bone health, and reduces metabolic risk factors. However, treating the female body like a male body — the "more, more often, harder" approach — can backfire in women, because the female hormonal system responds to energy deficits and stress loads differently from the male.

In this article I want to discuss two things. First: getting to know the four phases of your menstrual cycle. Second: adjusting your training according to those phases. Science has learned a great deal about this in the last decade; now it is time to translate that knowledge into practice.

**The four phases of the cycle**

The menstrual cycle consists of four phases, and your hormonal and energy state differs in each.

Menstrual phase (days 1–5, i.e. during menstruation). Oestrogen and progesterone are at their lowest. Energy is generally low and fatigue due to iron loss is possible. During this phase the body wants to rest.

Follicular phase (days 6–13). Oestrogen rises. Energy increases, mood improves, endurance and strength are at their best. You are open to trying new things, to intensive training, to setting records.

Ovulatory phase (around day 14, ovulation). Oestrogen peaks and testosterone rises slightly. These are the few days when you feel strongest. HIIT, resistance training, racing — all ideal.

Luteal phase (days 15–28). Progesterone rises; in the second half, oestrogen falls. Energy slowly decreases and cortisol sensitivity increases. Intensive training feels harder in this phase; moderate-intensity work is more productive.

**Training by phase**

Translating this knowledge into practice is not complicated.

During the follicular and ovulatory phases — roughly days 2 to 14 of the cycle — your body is most primed for high intensity. You will set your personal records in resistance training during this window. HIIT, sprinting, and intensive group classes are productive now. If you are preparing for a race, try to schedule your peak training weeks to coincide with these phases.

In the luteal phase — especially in the final week — it makes sense to reduce training intensity. This does not mean stopping; moderate-paced walking, yoga, Pilates, and light resistance training continue. If you work very intensively during this phase, performance drops and cortisol increases worsen PMS symptoms.

In the menstrual phase — during your period — ask your body what it wants. Some women prefer gentle yoga, walking, or light swimming; others say staying active reduces their period pain. This is individual; learn your own pattern. The general principle: "movement yes, racing no."

**Overtraining and loss of periods**

Let's return to my patient's story. Running long distances five days a week can create a serious energy deficit in the female body. When the body perceives this deficit, it puts the reproductive system into protective mode — that is to say, it shuts it down. This is called functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea.

Symptoms: the period starts arriving late, then is skipped, then disappears. Clinically, three consecutive months without a period is generally sufficient for a diagnosis of amenorrhoea. But the practical reality is this: a woman who has missed more than three periods in a row has reached the point where a conversation is needed.

The cause is usually a combination of three factors: excessive training, inadequate nutrition (particularly low fat intake), and chronic stress. This triad has been identified in female athletes and is known as RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport). It is a serious medical concern in Olympic-level athletes and affects not only performance but bone health, cardiovascular health, and reproductive capacity.

The good news: when caught early, it is reversible. When training intensity is reduced and nutrition is corrected, the body senses energy abundance again and the period returns within a few months. The first step, without self-reproach, is to recognise the problem.

**Nutrition — the essentials for the female athlete**

For women who exercise, nutrition is more sensitive than for male athletes. The female hormonal system is the first to respond to insufficient nutrition.

Carbohydrates — enough of them. Women who exercise often try low-carbohydrate diets for extended periods and most end up with menstrual irregularity. For women doing high-intensity training, 3–5 g/kg of carbohydrates per day (i.e. 180–300 g for a 60-kg woman) is generally adequate. Rather than demonising carbohydrates, the goal is to obtain them from the right sources: wholegrains, root vegetables, fruit, oats.

Fats — enough of them. Low-fat diets disrupt female hormone production because oestrogen and testosterone are synthesised from fat. Avocado, olive oil, nuts, oily fish — these are non-negotiable elements.

Iron — especially for menstruating women. Those who eat meat generally receive enough; for vegetarian athletes iron supplementation is advisable. Low iron is the silent cause of fatigue and diminished performance.

Protein — for muscle repair. For women who exercise, 1.2–1.6 g/kg per day is sufficient. Higher values may be appropriate in some contexts but are generally not necessary for women.

**A three-legged stool**

Good sport, good sleep, good menstruation — these three are not separate things. They are like a three-legged stool. When one leg breaks, the other two collapse as well.

I see this in my patients consistently. Women who organise their training, structure their nutrition, and prioritise sleep find that their periods correct themselves. A woman who neglects one of these gradually sees the others deteriorate too. Your body is a whole; listen to it as a whole.

> ***Doctor's note ---** Missing three consecutive periods is a personal health matter, not merely a problem with sportswear. The triad of excessive training, inadequate nutrition, and chronic stress is the greatest silent risk for female athletes. If things are regular, I am pleased; if they have become irregular, let's talk.*

**1.5**

**Your first job is not here to harm you — it's here to teach you who you are**

*The first year of a career is usually the year the body is most forgotten.*

> *A twenty-four-year-old patient came in during the third month of her first job: "My skin, my hair, my period — all deteriorating." They look like three separate things but share one cause: cortisol. "What did you change in three months?" I asked. "My life," she said.*

The transition from university to working life is a test period for a woman's body. The rules of time change, diet deteriorates, sleep shortens, the social structure rebuilds itself, dress codes, email hours, authority relationships — all at once. This intense adaptation process causes cortisol levels to remain persistently elevated. And when cortisol stays high in the female body, hormonal balance is the first thing affected.

In this article I want to discuss what happens physically during the first year of a career, what requires attention, and how to stay on your feet without losing yourself. Because the first year does not have to be a sprint that ends in burnout.

**Cortisol — the body's alarm hormone**

Cortisol is the hormone secreted by the adrenal glands that keeps the body in "alarm-on" mode. In short-term stress situations — an exam, an important presentation, a physical threat — cortisol primes the body for performance. This is healthy and necessary.

The problem is when cortisol remains chronically elevated. This happens very commonly in the first working year: a new environment, new responsibilities, constant feedback, the pressure of building a social network, financial stress — all accumulating to keep cortisol persistently high.

Effects of chronically elevated cortisol: skin problems (acne, dryness), hair loss, weight changes (particularly around the abdomen), sleep disorders, menstrual irregularities, persistent fatigue, anxiety, difficulty concentrating. If you recognise yourself in this list you are not alone; the majority of women in their first working year experience at least three of these symptoms.

**First-year traps**

The most common physical mistakes in the first working year are as follows.

Excess coffee. Filling morning energy deficits and afternoon slumps with coffee becomes a habit. A twenty-four-year-old woman drinking four to five cups of filter coffee a day may not notice at first, but after six months she begins to experience increased anxiety, sleep disorders, and palpitations. Caffeine tolerance is individual, but 200–300 mg a day (two to three cups) is the upper limit for most people.

Skipped meals. Breakfast is skipped, lunch becomes a sandwich, and "compensation" happens at home in the evening. This pattern creates insulin resistance, blood sugar swings, and an evening overeating cycle. The body is in a constant hunger–satiety race, which in turn elevates cortisol.

Late bedtimes, early wake-ups. A twenty-four-year-old woman needs seven to nine hours of sleep. It is possible to continue on five to six hours for months, but the physical cost accumulates: hormonal regulation deteriorates, immunity weakens, mood becomes unstable. The physical cost takes six to twelve months to become visible; when it does, it feels as though "everything suddenly got worse."

Communication pressure. Constant emails, messages, meetings — the brain is permanently switched on. This is not merely psychological fatigue; it is somatic tension. Shoulders, neck, jaw muscles, headaches — the body holds this pressure. Coming home in the evening is supposed to mean relaxing, but cortisol is still elevated; if you don't put the phone down before bed, you take it to bed with you.

**Not a solution — a ritual**

The solution is not "manage your stress better." That sentence is meaningless and makes people feel guilty. The solution is building small, repeated, protected windows.

Build your morning window. Fifteen to twenty minutes that belong to you before work. This window is not social media; it is not email. A cup of tea, a short walk, a page of a journal, breakfast sitting at the table. This window prevents cortisol from rising too sharply at the start of the day.

Build your evening window. A pause after work, on the transition home. A 20-minute walk, a shower, a glass of water, sitting and breathing. This window creates a biological buffer between work and home. Not taking your phone into the bedroom is the last step of that window.

These are not grand lifestyle changes — they are small daily practices. But when applied consistently, their effects are significant. After three months, your skin, your periods, and your mood will all register the difference.

**Nutritional foundations**

There are three fundamental rules for nutrition during a career phase.

Don't skip breakfast. Not coffee and something sweet in the morning — a protein-containing breakfast (eggs, cheese, yogurt) balances your blood sugar throughout the day and prevents the afternoon crash. This simple change produces a noticeable improvement in energy levels within three months.

Consider magnesium supplementation. A body under constant stress burns through magnesium quickly. 200–400 mg of magnesium citrate in the evening supports both sleep and muscle tension. Food sources: dark chocolate, almonds, pumpkin seeds, spinach.

Don't neglect fats and omega-3. A fat-free diet disrupts the female hormonal system. Avocado, olive oil, oily fish (two to three times a week), nuts — these should be on the table regularly.

**Where is the line?**

"Is this normal, or time for a doctor?" is a common question. A specialist's opinion makes sense in the following situations.

Menstrual irregularity persisting for six months — skipping, late arrival, very short or very long cycles.

Persistent fatigue — not resolved by sleep, difficulty getting up in the morning, a constant need for coffee throughout the day.

Increased anxiety — particularly a new anxiety in areas where you previously felt good (social situations, simple decisions).

Sleep disorders — difficulty falling asleep, waking during the night, not feeling rested upon waking.

When these occur individually they can be resolved; but when several appear together, professional support (gynaecologist, internist, psychiatry) saves time. Early intervention is better than looking back on months of unnecessary suffering.

> ***Doctor's note ---** What your first job is supposed to teach you is not "work harder" — it is "know your own limits." Your body is the teacher of those limits — but only if you listen. Even when all test results are normal, persistent fatigue may point to something not yet captured in the labs. A single appointment is enough to look there.*

**1.6**

**The first long relationship — body, intimacy, boundaries**

*Intimacy is not established once — it is continuously negotiated.*

> *A twenty-six-year-old patient asked: "What should I ask about my body in a relationship?" The words didn't come all at once. "What shouldn't I do, what should I ask, how should I speak?" — all of it is a language that is learned.*

The first long relationship means learning two things simultaneously for most women. First: living closely with another person. Second: living closely with your own body. These two are learned in parallel; without one, the other remains incomplete.

Sexual health is particularly under-discussed in Turkish. Phrases such as "know your body" and "set your limits" circulate widely but their concrete meaning is rarely provided. In this article — not as a medical guide but as topics discussed in premium clinical care — I want to address body, intimacy, and boundaries in concrete terms.

**Sexual health ≠ sexuality**

Two concepts are frequently confused.

Sexuality: sexual intercourse, sexual desire, sexual identity. This is a personal and cultural domain.

Sexual health: the biological state of sexual activity and the reproductive system. Vaginal health, protective measures, sexually transmitted infections (STIs), contraceptive choices, pain status, hygiene practices. This is a clinical domain and sharing information about it is entirely normal.

Whether you wish to discuss your sexuality is up to you. Sexual health, however — particularly in an active relationship — needs to be discussed. Otherwise you are making decisions about your health without the information you need.

**Contraception — getting to know your options**

Contraceptive options are clinical decisions and I will not cover them in detail here — there is a detailed article in the Knowledge Centre's General Gynaecology section. Here I will only say this: knowing your options is a right. Learning only one option (most commonly the oral contraceptive pill or a condom) is not sufficient. The pill, condoms, intrauterine devices (IUDs), implants, the calendar method, sterilisation — each has different advantages and disadvantages. Which is right for you is a clinical decision made together with your doctor. It cannot be made alone from the internet.

Knowing the right to emergency contraception (the morning-after pill) is also important. This is an option that can be taken within 72 hours (in some formulations 120 hours) after unprotected intercourse to prevent pregnancy. It is sold without a prescription at pharmacies. It is not a regular contraceptive method — it is an emergency option; but knowing it exists in an emergency is vital information.

**Vaginal health — important but under-discussed**

For a woman with an active sexual life, vaginal health is part of the daily routine.

Vaginal pH is acidic, between 3.8 and 4.5; this is maintained by the production of Lactobacillus bacteria. Three main factors disrupt this pH: excessive vaginal douching (with soapy or scented products), antibiotic use, and certain sexual practices. When the pH is disrupted, bacterial vaginosis or a yeast infection takes hold.

For protection: wash only the external genital area (the vulva) with water — no soap inside. "Intimate hygiene products" are not necessary; they often cause harm. Choose cotton underwear and bras; synthetic fabrics trap moisture.

Urinating after sexual intercourse significantly reduces the risk of urinary tract infection. This is a simple habit but highly effective.

If lubrication is needed, use a water-based lubricant. Inadequate lubrication is uncommon but possible in one's twenties — due to stress, hormonal change, or fatigue. It is not a cause for embarrassment; there is a solution.

**STI awareness**

Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) remain most prevalent in the twenty-something age group. Screening is recommended during this period.

This subject should be raised at annual gynaecology visits. Infections to be investigated include: HPV, chlamydia, gonorrhoea, herpes simplex, HIV, and hepatitis B and C. Most infections are treatable; but when unrecognised, their long-term consequences (infertility, cancer) are serious.

HPV falls into a special category. I would like to point to our Knowledge Centre here: in the Knowledge Centre (Knowledge Centres / HPV) all dimensions of this subject are covered in detail. Knowing that HPV exposure is common in one's twenties, and not reading it as "infidelity" or a "hygiene problem", is a mark of premium women's health awareness.

**The muscles of conversation**

Communicating boundaries is verbal before it is physical. Three basic sentence frameworks:

"My comfort zone is..." — saying what you like and what you are comfortable with is your right. This is not sexual knowledge — it is self-awareness.

"My boundary is..." — saying what you do not want is your right. Being able to say this sentence is an indicator of relational health; a relationship in which you cannot say it is not safe for you.

"I have the right to ask..." — your partner's history, health status, contraceptive choices. These questions are not a judgment — they are an exchange of information.

These sentences feel strange in the first attempts. With practice they become natural. And when they become natural, the foundation of a healthy relationship has been laid.

**Intimacy — continuous negotiation**

Intimacy is not established at the start of a relationship — it is continuously renewed. The boundaries you draw at the beginning of a relationship will change over time; this is natural. What matters is discussing the changes.

Unspoken limits are transgressed. Spoken limits — if your partner respects them — strengthen the foundation of the relationship. This rule applies to sexuality, to sharing, and to time.

Intimacy is reclaimed as a right, not given as a gift. Learning this in your twenties is the foundation you carry into all your relationships in your thirties, forties, and fifties.
