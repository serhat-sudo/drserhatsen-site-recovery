**2.1**

**Thirty-five — a biological fact or a cultural myth**

*The fertility window is real; but the "wall at 35" is a simplification of that reality.*

> *On the eve of her 34th birthday, a patient messages me: "Doctor, should I be panicking?" The question is not a simple one — is the "age-35 wall" real? The answer deserves more than a headline.*

Most women in their thirties in Turkey have heard the phrase: "It gets harder after 35." That statement is both true and false. True, because fertility does begin to decline from 35. False, because the word "wall" misrepresents reality; fertility follows a curve, not a cliff edge.

This article addresses two things: what the science actually says, and how to plan in light of it. Fear is not a prescription; knowledge is.

**Ovarian reserve — the mathematics of egg count**

A woman is born with approximately one to two million eggs. By puberty this number has fallen to roughly 300,000–500,000. Throughout the reproductive years, only one or two eggs mature each month, but hundreds more enter a process of natural attrition called atresia. By age 30, approximately 12–15% of the original reserve remains; by 40, around 3–5%.

Quality matters as much as quantity. From age 35, the rate of chromosomal abnormality in eggs begins to rise. At 30, chromosomal abnormality in embryos runs at roughly 30%; by 40, it reaches 60–70%. This is why "do I have enough eggs?" matters as much as "how healthy are my eggs?"

The practical reality: the monthly probability of conception is approximately 20–25% up to age 35, about 15% between 35 and 37, around 10% between 38 and 40, and approximately 5% after 40. These statistics do not reflect individual variation; some women conceive naturally at 42, others struggle at 32. An individual clinical assessment always takes precedence over population statistics.

**The AMH test — understanding its limits**

Anti-Müllerian Hormone (AMH) is the biochemical marker of ovarian reserve. It is measured with a simple blood test and has become popular among women in their late thirties. But AMH has things it can and cannot tell you.

What AMH tells you: the status of your ovarian reserve relative to your age. A low AMH (age-adjusted) may signal the risk of early menopause.

What AMH does not tell you: your chance of conceiving. A woman with low AMH can still conceive naturally; a woman with high AMH may struggle. AMH reflects egg quantity, not quality.

For this reason, AMH alone should not be a source of panic. Rushing to IVF because you heard "low AMH," or deferring pregnancy plans because you heard "normal AMH," are both incorrect responses. AMH needs to be interpreted as one component of a broader clinical picture.

A single test should not define your fertility plan. In addition to AMH, the antral follicle count (AFC — the number of small follicles visible on ultrasound), FSH, and estradiol values complete a proper clinical assessment.

**Egg freezing — a gift of timing**

Social egg freezing (oocyte cryopreservation) has become an increasingly accessible option in Turkey over the past decade. For women who do not yet have a partner, or who wish to defer pregnancy, freezing eggs while young — and using them when needed — can be thought of as a form of insurance.

Practical realities: the ideal age is 30–34. Success rates for eggs frozen after 35 decline; freezing after 38 generally offers diminishing returns.

The process: 10–14 days of hormonal stimulation, followed by a brief surgical procedure (egg retrieval). The total time commitment is one to two weeks. An average cycle yields 10–15 eggs; at least 15–20 are recommended for a reasonable chance of a healthy pregnancy, which means more than one cycle is often necessary.

Cost in Turkey ranges from approximately 50,000–80,000 TL per cycle, with annual storage costs of 5,000–10,000 TL.

Expectation management is critical: some frozen eggs may not survive thawing, not all that survive will fertilise, and not all that fertilise will result in pregnancy. "I froze my eggs, I have a guarantee" is not realistic; "I improved my chances" is the more accurate frame.

**The male factor — the quiet reality**

In fertility conversations, the woman's age dominates; the man's age receives far less attention. Yet recent data shows that paternal age is also a meaningful variable.

After 40, sperm quality declines and sperm DNA fragmentation rates increase. This both lengthens the time to conception and modestly raises the risk of miscarriage. Paternal age has also been identified as a risk factor in neurodevelopmental conditions including autism and schizophrenia in recent research.

This makes fertility planning a shared decision. Your partner's age, health, smoking and alcohol habits, and nutrition are all part of the picture. Placing responsibility solely on the woman is both unfair and clinically incomplete.

**A decision framework — plan, not panic**

In your thirties, fertility planning involves three possible scenarios.

One: I want to conceive soon. In that case, seeking medical support after six to twelve months of trying without success is sensible. For couples under 30, a year of trying is standard; over 35, an evaluation after six months is appropriate.

Two: I am thinking about children in the medium term (three to five years). In that case, learning your reserve status through AMH and clinical assessment is worthwhile; if results are low, egg freezing can be discussed sooner rather than later.

Three: I am not sure when or if. In that case, annual gynecological check-ups continue; AMH is assessed between ages 32 and 34; decisions take shape over time. Clear information facilitates clear decisions.

None of these three scenarios is wrong. What matters is making any decision consciously. "I won't think about it now" is also a decision — it just becomes meaningful when made knowingly.

> ***Dr. note —** 35 is not a wall — it is a turning point. It is time to plan, not time to panic. Learning your AMH value does not mean you must do something — it means seeing your map. Setting out without a map is no more of an option than not looking at the one in your hands.*

**2.2**

**Skin in the 30s — the oil shifts, the light shifts, you don't**

*Skin change has a medical explanation; the feelings it provokes lie beyond medicine.*

> *A 32-year-old patient comes in and wipes off her makeup: "Doctor, I used to go out without makeup. I don't recognise myself anymore." Her skin has changed — but the real turning point is the moment she notices that makeup had become her reason to look in the mirror.*

In the thirties, skin changes on two fronts: the biological and the perceptual. The biological front consists of the concrete differences created by collagen loss and hormonal shifts. The perceptual front is how we notice these changes, how often we look in the mirror, which faces we compare ourselves to on social media. The two reinforce each other.

In this article I want to discuss not just what to do, but why things change. Because when you understand what is shifting and why, you can make conscious choices rather than panic.

**Five fronts of skin change in your 30s**

Sebum production decreases. The oil production that gave skin its glow in the twenties slows; skin appears drier and more matte. The benefit of oily skin — moisture retention, radiance — gradually fades.

Dehydration increases. Natural hyaluronic acid production falls; skin retains less moisture. Fine lines — especially under the eyes and around the mouth — appear more visible on waking.

Pigmentation emerges. Accumulated sun damage over the years, combined with hormonal changes (particularly pregnancy or oral contraceptive use), can produce uneven tone known as melasma. These are distinct from the moles you have always had.

Cell turnover slows. The epidermal renewal cycle that took 28 days in the twenties extends to 35–40 days in the thirties. This is why the skin appears dull and tired.

Fine lines begin to settle. Particularly around the eyes, mouth, and forehead — "expression lines," the traces of years of repeated facial movement. At 32, "invisible"; at 35, "noticed"; at 39, "accepted" — that is often the journey.

**Estrogen and skin — a brief biology**

Estrogen supports collagen production, keeps the skin plump, and preserves moisture. In the thirties, estrogen levels remain normal but fluctuations within the cycle become more pronounced. Some women experience noticeable skin changes before and after their period — this is normal.

In women on the contraceptive pill, skin tends to stay stable; when the pill is stopped, a one-to-three month adjustment period in the skin follows. In pregnant women, a radiant complexion in the second trimester is common; melasma may increase in the third.

Knowing this hormonal context makes it easier to read skin changes in terms of cause and effect. "My skin just went wrong for no reason" becomes "I'm going through a particular hormonal shift right now" — a framing that brings calm rather than alarm.

**Actives to add**

Four key actives belong in your skin routine in your thirties.

Retinol, used at night. A vitamin A derivative, retinol stimulates collagen synthesis, accelerates cell renewal, and reduces fine lines. It is the only anti-aging active with robust evidence behind it. Start with a low concentration (0.25% or 0.3%), two to three evenings a week. Dose and frequency increase as the skin adapts.

Vitamin C, used in the morning. An antioxidant that reduces sun damage, evens skin tone, and supports collagen production. Formulations of 10–20% L-ascorbic acid are typically effective. Applied to clean skin in the morning, followed by moisturiser, then SPF.

Peptide serums. Stimulate the skin's collagen synthesis. Can be used morning or evening; should be applied at a different time from retinol.

Niacinamide (vitamin B3). Strengthens the skin barrier, reduces pore appearance, and has an anti-inflammatory effect. Particularly valuable in acne-prone skin. Well-tolerated across virtually all skin types.

**Aesthetic interventions — "when is too early"**

The question of aesthetic intervention comes up in the thirties. There is a genuinely contested area here: "Is this too early?" The scientific answer: preventive intervention between 35 and 38 is meaningful. More aggressive interventions at younger ages can sometimes create an appearance of premature aging, because the body carries the intervention for a long time.

Botox has a reasonable indication in the thirties — particularly for pronounced expression lines in the forehead and glabella (between the brows). However, the "frozen face" result must be avoided; conservative dosing and correct injection points are critical.

Fillers are generally more appropriate after 35. Lip or cheek filler before 30 often creates an artificial appearance. Allowing natural maturation to proceed is a valid conscious choice.

Laser and microneedling may be a priority in the thirties — especially for pigmentation, enlarged pores, or acne scarring. Fractional laser resurfaces the skin and stimulates collagen production.

The question to ask before any intervention: "Where did I hear about this, what do I want it to achieve, what outcome am I confident I want?" Seeing real-world results rather than social media filters, and working with a clinically experienced physician, come first.

**Embracing natural aging**

While discussing skin routines and aesthetic options, one sentence must not be lost: aging is not a bad thing. On the editorial pages of Vogue Italia and Casa Vogue, women at 40, 50, and 60 — with their lines, their grey hair, their lived-in faces — appear as icons of premium beauty.

In Turkey, this culture of acceptance has not yet fully matured; "looking young" is presented as obligatory. Yet in premium culture the priority has shifted toward "aging well" — not erasing the signs of age, but sustaining skin that is healthy, full, and radiant.

This philosophy changes your routine. You intervene, but not to "reset your age" — to "keep your skin healthy." You consider Botox, but conservative rather than aggressive. You think about filler, but for "volume restoration" rather than "inflation." The philosophy does not change the options; it changes how you choose among them.

> ***Dr. note —** Skin change is a mirror reminding you that you have not changed. Offer knowledge, not judgment. Every skin is beautiful not in comparison to what it was, but in what it is right now. Carrying that beauty — with a conscious routine, the right actives, and professional support when needed — is in your hands. Skin health is a mirror of overall health; if your skin is stubbornly troublesome while your bloodwork is normal, a hormonal assessment is worth opening.*

**2.3**

**Mother and doctor — carrying two identities**

*When a child is born, a new woman is born — the former woman does not disappear.*

> *A 36-year-old patient, six months after her first birth: "Doctor, I still don't know who I am." Medicine does not resolve that sentence — but what I have learned is this: it was usually an unasked question. Once spoken, it begins to resolve.*

Motherhood is one of the most profound transformations in a woman's life. Hormonally, socially, and in terms of identity. The biological side of this transformation appears in medical textbooks — the postpartum period, lochia, lactation, pelvic floor recovery. The identity side is rarely discussed, because discussing it can be read as "not being grateful enough."

This article addresses both the hormonal foundation and the identity experience. Because the two are not separate — without one, the other remains incomplete.

**Postpartum identity shift — real and normal**

The first six months after birth is the period in which a woman redefines who she is. Do not read that sentence as a grand hypothesis — it has real hormonal and neurological foundations.

Within hours of delivery, estrogen and progesterone levels plummet dramatically. No other phase in a woman's life involves a hormonal shift this sharp or this rapid. This change directly affects mood, emotional resilience, and sense of identity.

Simultaneously, prolactin and oxytocin rise — supporting breastfeeding and bonding. But these hormones also shift "focused attention"; new mothers become highly attuned to their infant's needs while becoming less responsive to their own. This is an evolutionary adaptation — the infant's survival requires the mother's attention to be redirected.

Brain imaging studies show that the female brain undergoes measurable structural changes in the postpartum period — particularly in areas associated with empathy and social cognition, which expand. The "maternal brain" is real; it is not a passing feeling but a measurable biological transformation.

New mothers feel like "a different person" for exactly this reason. Because they are, in fact, a different person — not only emotionally but biologically. Recognising this difference, and meeting the new self rather than mourning the old one, is the core work of the postpartum period.

**Three-cornered fatigue**

Postpartum exhaustion has three corners.

Hormonal drop — as described above. The first three months are the most intense; if breastfeeding, a second drop occurs around the sixth month (during weaning).

Sleep deprivation — a newborn wakes every two to three hours. In the first three months, average sleep is five to six hours in fragmented segments; this is chronic sleep deprivation, and it elevates cortisol, disrupts mood, and weakens immunity. This sleep debt does not recover all at once; sleep quality returns gradually as the child grows.

Social expectation — "you should be happy," "you should be grateful," "motherhood is the most beautiful feeling" — these sentences suppress the new mother's real experience. A mother feeling exhausted and confused cannot say so, because she does not want to appear ungrateful. This silence lays the groundwork for postpartum depression.

Each of these three corners is manageable on its own. When all three converge — as they do for new mothers — exhaustion gets reclassified as "normal motherhood." But it is a situation that calls for asking for help.

**Questioning the motherhood instinct myth**

"A mother knows by instinct" — this sentence looks beautiful but is not uniformly supported by science. Mothering is partly biological (oxytocin, prolactin), partly cultural (learned from one's own mother and society), and partly personal (every baby is different).

The conscious awareness every new mother deserves to hold is this: "I am not feeling this right now — that does not make me a bad mother." The bonding feeling is not required to arrive in the delivery room; for some mothers it may take weeks or months. This delay — if mother and baby are both well — can be a sign of postpartum depression and must be discussed.

The word "instinct" does a disservice to mothers. Because when you do not know, it implies that you were supposed to know instinctively. But mothering is something learned — on the first day, in the first month, in the first year, and throughout every year that follows. Accepting this reduces the pressure.

**Communication as a couple**

When you become a mother, your relationship with your partner changes. Before, you were two adults; now you are two adults and a baby. New roles, new division of labour, new stresses. This transition is hard, and couples rarely discuss it enough.

Two major areas of tension emerge.

The first: division of labour. A new mother's work is often "invisible" — infant care, household management, mental load ("the vaccinations are due this month," "the nappies are running low," "there's a doctor's appointment tomorrow"). The partner perceives this as "waiting mode" and underestimates their own contribution. This quiet imbalance quietly becomes resentment.

The second: changes in sexual life. Libido typically decreases in the postpartum period — for hormonal reasons, fatigue, body image, and the suppressive effect of prolactin during breastfeeding. This can be read as "loss," when in reality it is a temporary phase. When couples cannot discuss it openly, both feel alone.

Early conversation — asking at three months "how are we feeling right now, what are we sharing, what do we need to say?" — strengthens the relational foundation for the years ahead.

**The inheritance between mother and daughter**

When you become a mother yourself, your relationship with your own mother also changes. You begin to think about what you received from her mothering, what you want to carry forward, and what you want to do differently. This is a conscious generational turning point.

Which of her practices will you keep? Which words will you choose not to say to your child — because they were said to you and they stung? Which traditions will you continue because you loved them? These questions appear suddenly in the postpartum period — in the moment your baby cries, in how you feed them, in the first words you say to them.

This awareness is a form of power. Raising a child is not repetition — it is selection. Every generation of mothers learns from the one before and chooses differently. That right of selection is worth reflecting on for a long time.

> ***Dr. note —** Being a mother is part of a life — not the entirety of a life — and that sentence is a permissible one. Persistent heaviness after six postpartum weeks, a sustained tendency to cry, an inability to experience motherhood as any kind of pleasure — these may be signs of postpartum depression. They cannot be overcome alone; a gynecology–psychiatry partnership changes everything. Motherhood is not a reason for shame — it is a reason to reach for professional support.*

**2.4**

**The stress test — there is no prescription, because you are the prescription**

*In the 30s, cortisol, fertility, skin, sleep, and mood all drink from the same pool. The pool is called: stress management.*

> *A 37-year-old patient. "No matter what I do, I'm exhausted." Bloodwork clean. "I want something — a pill, a vitamin, a prescription." "What has no prescription can still be a prescription," I said. She looked at me — she did not like that answer. Then she tried it. Three months later she came back: "It worked."*

The thirties are the decade of the tired woman. Career, partner, children, home, social life — all adult responsibilities arriving at once. In these intense years, cortisol becomes a quiet agent reshaping a woman's health. Not one bad day, but chronic pressure sustained across years.

In this article we will discuss cortisol's effects on the female body, the "to-do list syndrome" of the thirties, and why the prescription must be personal.

**The cortisol–insulin–estrogen triangle**

Three hormones move together in the female body: cortisol (stress), insulin (blood sugar), estrogen (female rhythm). These three are interconnected.

When cortisol is chronically elevated, it increases insulin resistance. Insulin resistance causes the pancreas to produce more insulin to stabilise blood sugar. High insulin stimulates androgen (male-hormone-like) production in the ovaries. High androgen disrupts the estrogen–progesterone balance.

The result: irregular periods, acne, excess hair, weight changes, fertility difficulties — all potentially arising from a single cycle of chronically elevated cortisol.

The practical implication: if a woman in her thirties is experiencing skin problems, menstrual irregularity, or weight changes labelled a "hormonal disorder," the root cause deserves examination. Evaluating the rhythm of daily life before starting a hormonal medication may resolve the problem entirely.

**The "to-do list" syndrome**

Most women in their thirties spend a significant portion of their mental bandwidth running through their internal list. "Get up early, get the child ready, don't be late for work, meeting, doctor's appointment, call Mum, reply to emails, prepare something for dinner, clear the dishes, put the child to bed, talk to your partner." The list never ends.

This is not a sign of high achievement — it is a sign of chronically elevated cortisol. The brain is permanently in "next thing" mode. It never fully rests. Physically, this manifests as tension settling into the shoulders, jaw, stomach region.

The premium burnout audit uses three questions: 1) Has there been a moment recently where you gave time to yourself — truly only to yourself? 2) When you finish something, does it feel like you gained something, or does the next item immediately start? 3) If I were to fall ill, what would happen to my list? Honest answers to these three make the seriousness of the situation clear.

**Not solutions — rituals**

"Manage your stress" is a meaningless sentence. Because managing stress is not like taking a pill — it is the cumulative effect of small daily repetitions. The prescription needs to shift from "what will I do" to "which window will I protect."

Morning window: 30 minutes. A space that belongs only to you before work begins. Not the phone, not email. A coffee, a passage from a book, a short walk. This window prevents cortisol from spiking too sharply at the start of the day. Over years, the difference accumulates.

Evening window: 30 minutes. A pause between leaving work and arriving home. A walk, a shower, even sitting in the car for a few minutes. This window prevents the day's pressure from being carried into the home relationship. You appear before your child and partner not as "a different person" but as "someone who took a moment for themselves before crossing the threshold."

These windows are not grand lifestyle overhauls. They are small, regular, protected spaces. But applied consistently, their physical effects become noticeable within three to six months.

**Nutrition fundamentals**

The body's core needs in a high-cortisol environment are clear.

Protein at every meal. Even at breakfast. Eggs, cheese, yoghurt, lentils — adding one to every meal stabilises blood sugar and prevents the mid-day energy crash.

Magnesium is rapidly depleted by chronically elevated cortisol. 200–400 mg of magnesium citrate or glycinate in the evening supports both sleep and muscle tension relief. Food sources: dark chocolate, almonds, pumpkin seeds, dark leafy greens.

Omega-3 (salmon, sardines, walnuts, flaxseed) dampens the inflammatory response and supports brain health. Two to three portions of oily fish per week, or a daily tablespoon of flaxseed, is sufficient.

A B-vitamin complex can be supportive under sustained stress. B6, B9, and B12 are particularly important for mood and energy.

Caffeine tolerance is individual, but for most women in their thirties 200 mg per day (approximately two cups of filter coffee) is the upper limit. More simply amplifies anxiety and sleep problems when cortisol is already high.

**Movement — not therapy, a choice**

Movement in the thirties should be framed not as therapy but as a daily choice.

Yoga — particularly Hatha or Yin Yoga — activates the parasympathetic system ("rest and digest"). Thirty minutes of yoga after an intense day produces a measurable reduction in cortisol.

Walking — 30–45 minutes daily, outdoors or in a park. This simple form of exercise offers evidence-based support for both cardiovascular health and mental wellbeing.

Strength training — two to three times per week. From the thirties, bone protection and muscle mass preservation become critical for women. Crossfit or intense fitness programmes are not required; resistance bands or a basic dumbbell routine at home is sufficient.

One of these three, or a combination of them — applied with daily consistency — can produce antidepressant-level effects. "Movement is medicine" is not a metaphor; it is scientifically correct.

**The line — when professional support is indicated**

Lifestyle regulation works for most women's stress management. But in some situations professional support is necessary. The following signs indicate this:

Persistent anxiety, nocturnal panic attacks.

Relational deterioration — increasing tension with a partner, child, or colleagues.

Somatic symptoms: persistent headaches, digestive problems, palpitations, inability to sleep through the night.

Thoughts of self-harm, or a deep sense of hopelessness.

If any of these are present, the time has come to seek psychiatric or psychological support alongside gynecological care. Hormonal and psychiatric support work together; without one, the other remains incomplete.

> ***Dr. note —** Stress management is not about deleting items from the calendar — it is about redrawing it. Replacing "I have this much to do" with "I have time for this much, the rest is for later" is a sentence worth learning in your thirties. The woman who cannot learn it pays the cost in her forties, when perimenopause arrives alongside.*

**2.5**

**The middle path in nutrition — not a diet, a rhythm**

*Diets come and go; rhythm stays.*

> *A 35-year-old patient greeted me with 16:8. Then keto. Then Mediterranean. "Which one is right?" she asked. "Which one is yours?" I asked back. A year later she returned — she had found her own version.*

Most women in their thirties have, at some point, followed a diet. Some for weight, some for health, some because a trend was hard to ignore. The result is usually the same: a change that holds for a few months, then the old pattern returns, with the body a little more depleted than before.

The reason is not a lack of willpower. The reason is treating the female body according to male nutritional science. Most dietary protocols derive from research conducted on male participants. The female hormonal system responds to energy deficits and macronutrient shifts differently. Knowing this difference makes it possible to move from diet fatigue to rhythmic health.

**The diet-fatigue phenomenon**

Most women in their thirties have tried at least three to five different approaches. This accumulation has consequences.

Chronic calorie restriction (particularly below 1,500 kcal) slows thyroid function in the female body, causes menstrual irregularity, and reduces metabolic rate over the long term. The cycle of repeated weight loss and regain known as "yo-yo dieting" leads to permanent metabolic adaptation.

Excessively low-fat diets disrupt female hormone production. Estrogen, progesterone, and testosterone are synthesised from fat. In women who sustain fat intake below 15% for extended periods, loss of period, reduced libido, and skin changes are typical.

Very low carbohydrate (particularly ketogenic) diets are not universally appropriate for women. Some tolerate them well, but in the majority cortisol elevation, menstrual irregularity, and thyroid slowing are observed. Men tolerate ketogenic approaches better; this is because the male body is less glucose-dependent than the female one.

This fatigue is biological, not a personal failing. The right question to ask the female body is not "which diet?" but "which rhythm?"

**Three principles**

Three core principles support hormonal health.

The first: protein at every meal. Breakfast, lunch, dinner. Eggs, cheese, yoghurt, lentils, meat, fish — even adding a small amount to vegetable dishes is enough. Protein stabilises blood sugar, prolongs satiety, and supports muscle mass. From the thirties, 1.2–1.6 g/kg per day is adequate for women; approximately 75–100 g for a 60 kg woman.

The second: keep glucose balanced. This does not mean "cut carbohydrates." The opposite: take the right carbohydrates at the right time. Pure sugar on an empty stomach in the morning (fruit juice, white bread) raises blood sugar quickly, then drops it sharply. Eating the same fruit alongside walnuts or almonds slows absorption. Whole grain + fat + protein at every meal maintains glucose balance.

The third: an early last meal. Eating should be finished two to three hours before bed. Late dinners disrupt sleep quality, make weight management harder, and place stress on the digestive system. 7–8 pm is ideal; in real life, around 9 pm is acceptable; 10 pm and later, if it is a regular habit, is worth correcting.

**The Mediterranean template — why it works for most women**

The Mediterranean dietary pattern is the most evidence-based of the past 30 years. It is not a trend — it is an ancient model. Its characteristics:

Abundant vegetables and fruit. Colour variety at every meal. Rich in fibre, antioxidants, and micronutrients.

Whole grains. Bulgur, quinoa, oats, whole wheat. Instead of white flour.

Olive oil as the primary fat. Unsaturated fatty acids, antioxidants, anti-inflammatory properties.

Regular fish and seafood. Two to three times per week. Omega-3 and quality protein.

Limited red meat. Once or twice a week is sufficient. Chicken and fish as primary proteins.

Legumes regularly. Lentils, chickpeas, beans. Plant protein, fibre, and minerals.

Dairy products in moderation. Yoghurt, cheese; sweet dairy products limited.

Minimal processed food. Ready meals, fast food, and packaged snacks as rarely as possible.

This list is not new — it is the Mediterranean kitchen of thousands of years. A sustainable, meaningful foundation that supports hormonal health in the thirties.

**Intermittent fasting + female hormones — proceed carefully**

Intermittent fasting has become popular over the past decade. It showed metabolic benefits in male-participant research. In the female body, the picture is more complex.

Short-window IF (12–14 hour fast) is well-tolerated by most women. This is a natural interval — last meal at 7 pm, breakfast at 7 am. Most people already live this way; it simply becomes conscious.

Longer windows (16:8 or 18:6) cause menstrual irregularity, thyroid slowing, and skin changes in some women. If these symptoms emerge, shortening the window is the right step.

Very long windows (24+ hours) are generally not recommended for women. Cortisol spikes and hormonal fluctuations become pronounced.

Individual variation is large. Some women can sustain 16:8 as a long-term lifestyle; others are negatively affected within weeks. Your body will tell you — you just have to listen.

**Stepping off the scale**

In your thirties, "weight" looks like the easiest measurement. But it is not the only or most accurate one.

The right measurements are these.

Energy levels — how do you feel in the morning, at midday, in the evening? If persistent fatigue is present, something other than weight needs discussing.

Sleep quality — can you sleep deeply? Do you feel rested on waking? Good sleep is a sign of good metabolism.

Cycle regularity — is it regular, painful, flowing, or irregular? When nutrition deteriorates, the cycle is the first to react.

Skin and hair — healthy and radiant, or dry and shedding? A mirror of hormonal and micronutrient status.

Bodily sense — how do your clothes fit, how do you feel in front of a mirror? A far more important indicator than a number on a scale.

If these five are in good shape, the number on the scale is not important. If they are not all good, weight loss alone will not be enough either.

> ***Dr. note —** Weight is one parameter; hormones, sleep, energy, cycle, and skin are many more. Fixating on a single number does your body a disservice. Replacing "how much weight did I lose?" with "how do I feel?" is a question worth learning in your thirties.*

**2.6**

**"Second spring" in a relationship — the biology of deepening**

*The biology of deepening sexual life exists; the cultural story is different.*

> *A couple in their late thirties comes in: "Something has changed." I did not begin with their questions — "When did you last ask each other something specific and concrete?" Silence. Then they began to talk.*

The late thirties is a critical juncture in long relationships. The early romantic period has passed; children, if present, are at the centre of life; careers are established. At this point, most couples experience the feeling that "something has changed." For some, this reads as the end of the relationship; for others, it can be the beginning of something new.

The evolution of sexual life is part of this shift. In this article we will discuss what is happening on both the biological and relational fronts. Because body and emotion are not separate; when addressed together, "second spring" becomes a realistic possibility.

**Libido + estrogen + testosterone in the thirties**

A woman's libido in her thirties is affected by several hormonal shifts.

Estrogen levels remain normal in the thirties but fluctuations within the cycle become more pronounced. Libido peaks at ovulation and falls before and during menstruation. This cyclical variation is normal; a woman who is unaware of it may interpret it as "my libido has disappeared," when in fact she is simply in a particular phase of the cycle.

Testosterone — produced in women as well — begins to decline slightly from the late thirties. Testosterone is important for libido, energy, and mood. Low testosterone symptoms: decreased sexual desire, persistent fatigue, loss of motivation.

The postpartum period — if breastfeeding, prolactin remains elevated and libido is suppressed. This lasts six months to a year and is normal. After weaning, libido gradually returns.

If you are using an oral contraceptive — some pills raise SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin), which lowers free testosterone and can reduce libido. If there is a clear connection between the start of a pill and libido change, an alternative contraceptive is worth considering.

**The stress–relationship–sexual life triangle**

The reality of the thirties: fatigue is the single greatest libido killer. Not hormonal cause — basic physiology.

A fatigued body is not open to sexual stimulation. When cortisol is high, prolactin is unbalanced, sleep is insufficient, and the mental load is at its peak, sexual desire retreats in self-protection. This is not the relationship's problem — it is the body's protective response.

Similarly, relationship quality directly affects sexual life. If you and your partner go through the day without a real conversation, being suddenly intimate at 10 pm is hard. Intimacy does not begin at 10 pm; it is built in the small connection moments throughout the day — a morning greeting, a message, sharing dinner together, coffee together.

These three — body, relationship, and sexual life — feed each other. When one is compromised, the other two follow.

**Vaginal health foundations**

In the thirties, vaginal health is generally stable, but some early changes may begin.

Lubrication — with fatigue, stress, or insufficient foreplay, natural lubrication may be inadequate. This does not mean hormonal loss. Normalising the use of water-based lubricant is an area where there is nothing to be embarrassed about — it is a practical tool.

Microbiota — vaginal flora is still healthy in the thirties, but antibiotic use, hormonal fluctuations, and hygiene products can cause harm. If you are experiencing recurrent yeast infections or bacterial vaginosis, lifestyle measures that support microbiota health (probiotics, appropriate underwear, avoiding vaginal douching) come first.

Sexual life with an IUD — an IUD does not interfere with sexual life; most women are unaware of it after insertion. If your partner says "I can feel it," the thread length should be checked and shortened if needed.

Pelvic floor — particularly postpartum, pelvic floor muscles may weaken. This can reduce sensitivity during sex. Pelvic floor exercises (Kegel and modern alternatives) strengthen these muscles; pelvic floor physiotherapy, when needed, provides highly valuable support.

**Communication muscles**

In long relationships, "not talking" is not actually the most common problem — "talking about the wrong things" is. In sexual life, complaints, criticism, and adjustment are frequently discussed, but open and non-judgmental dialogue is rare.

Three basic types of conversation matter.

The first: stating a need. "I have missed you." "If you are not tired, I would like to talk for a while." "My body needs to rest today." Direct expression rather than indirect.

The second: sharing experience. "I really enjoy that." "I would like to stop doing this." These sentences are not criticism — they are practical information.

The third: stating a boundary. "This is hard when I am tired." "I do not want to do this this week." Boundary sentences do not reject the relationship — they speak the truth.

These sentences feel artificial at first. Once they become habitual, they strengthen the relational foundation. Just as unused muscles atrophy, unused communication causes a "deadening" within the relationship.

**Couples therapy — breaking the stigma**

Couples therapy in Turkey is seen as something "troubled couples" do. In the premium world it is increasingly understood as something "healthy couples" do.

Going to therapy without a problem is a conscious act of care. Think of it like an annual check-up — checking before illness sets in. Couples typically seek therapy only when a serious problem has emerged, at which point the damage runs deeper.

Couples therapy does not only resolve conflict — it helps you identify shared goals, clarify communication patterns, and recognise harmful dynamics that have become habitual over the years. It is an infrastructure investment in a quality relationship.

There are skilled couples therapists in Turkey — respectful, well-trained, and experienced. Do not be embarrassed to ask for a recommendation. Finding the right therapist for your relationship is like finding the right doctor; if the first one does not fit, you try the second.

> ***Dr. note —** Sexual intimacy is a matter of the body, but it belongs first to the mind — when the two are not in conversation, a therapist rather than a doctor is the more appropriate first door. For physical symptoms — sexual pain, persistent loss of libido, vaginal dryness — the gynecologist's door is always open. But the answer to "what am I feeling?" is usually found faster in a therapy room.*
