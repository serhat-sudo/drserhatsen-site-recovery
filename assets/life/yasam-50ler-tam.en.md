**4.1**

**Menopause — it was one moment; who said it lasts a decade?**

*Menopause is the date of the last period; the menopausal years are the decade that ends with it.*

> *A 52-year-old patient: "I haven't had a period in two years. Am I in menopause?" Medical definition: yes. Practical reality: the ten-year transition has already been lived.*

Medically, menopause is a moment; culturally, it is a decade. Most women read menopause as "I've grown old," yet with rising life expectancy, the postmenopausal period now accounts for roughly one-third of a woman's life. Reading menopause not as "the beginning of the end" but as "the beginning of a new chapter" is both accurate and healthier.

In this article we will discuss the biological facts of menopause, the symptom spectrum, the updated evidence on hormone therapy, and lifestyle foundations. Because without accurate information, menopause can be a decade of unnecessary suffering.

**Menopause defined + the difference from perimenopause**

Perimenopause: the transition period leading to menopause. Ovarian function declines in an irregular, fluctuating pattern. Duration 3–10 years; onset typically around age 40–45.

Menopause: the moment defined as twelve months after the last period. In Turkey, the average age is 49–50.

Postmenopause: all of life after menopause. For a woman with a life expectancy in her eighties, this is 30+ years.

Early menopause: occurring between ages 40 and 45. May have genetic, autoimmune, or medical causes. Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI): before age 40, requiring close follow-up and treatment.

**The symptom spectrum**

Vasomotor symptoms — hot flashes, night sweats. Approximately 75% of women experience them; in 25% they significantly impact daily life. Average duration: 7–10 years.

Vaginal dryness and atrophy — increases over subsequent years. Detailed in the following article.

Sleep problems — REM sleep architecture changes, fatigue accumulates.

Mood changes — risk of depression and anxiety rises. Women with prior mental health concerns require particular attention.

Skin, hair, and joint changes — indirect effects of declining estrogen. Skin thins, hair may shed, joints may ache.

Cognitive changes — word-finding difficulty, memory lapses. Temporary in most women; stabilises within 2–3 years after menopause. "Am I developing Alzheimer's?" is a common worry; in most cases, the answer is no.

Libido — decreases in many women, but individual variation is very large. Some women report no change in libido — or even an increase. The absence of pregnancy risk and periods can be genuinely liberating.

**HRT — updated evidence**

Hormone replacement therapy has undergone a dramatic evidence revision in recent years.

In 2002, the results of the WHI study were misinterpreted. Headlines reading "HRT increases cancer and heart disease risk" dominated. As subsequent research deepened, the true picture emerged differently.

Current science: when HRT is commenced before age 60, uses modern formulations (transdermal estrogen + micronised progesterone), and is planned with appropriate indication — the benefit–risk balance is favourable for most women.

Benefits: dramatic reduction in vasomotor symptoms (the most effective treatment available), improvement in sleep, mood stabilisation, improvement in vaginal health, bone protection, and potential cardiovascular and neurocognitive protection.

Risks: a small increase in breast cancer risk with combined HRT, with long-term use — approximately 1–2 additional cases per 1,000 women per year. Women taking estrogen alone (after hysterectomy) do not have an increased breast cancer risk — it may even decrease slightly. Thrombosis risk is elevated for oral formulation but minimal for transdermal — which is why modern HRT generally favours the transdermal route.

Contraindications: active or recent breast cancer, other estrogen-sensitive cancers, unexplained vaginal bleeding, active thrombosis, severe liver disease, history of heart attack or stroke.

The decision is individual. Family history, personal health history, severity of symptoms, and personal preference are all discussed. Current science, with its renewed evidence, now strongly favours use for the right candidates.

**Botanical alternatives**

For women who do not want HRT or for whom it is contraindicated, botanical supplements exist. Soy isoflavones (phytoestrogen, modest effect on vasomotor symptoms), black cohosh (effective for hot flashes), red clover, maca root (libido and energy), Vitex agnus-castus (for perimenopausal cycle irregularity).

Botanical supplements should not be taken uncritically just because they are "natural." They have effects and interactions; speaking with a doctor first is essential.

**Lifestyle foundation**

Whether or not you take HRT, the lifestyle foundation remains important.

Strength training — two to three times per week, essential for bone protection. Yoga (Hatha or Yin) — cortisol regulation, sleep improvement, effect on hot flashes. Nutrition — Mediterranean base + calcium + vitamin D + omega-3 + phytoestrogen sources.

Sleep hygiene — bedroom cool, dark, quiet. Alcohol in the evening minimised; alcohol significantly worsens night sweats. Stress management — cortisol sensitivity is elevated. Social connection — loneliness emerges as a health risk factor in the postmenopausal period.

> ***Dr. note —** Menopause is not a problem — it is a transition. But a transition lived with the right guidance has its own dignity. Years of unnecessary suffering are not required; modern medicine has options, and all it takes is knowing them and speaking with your doctor.*

**4.2**

**Vaginal health post-menopause — a silent subject with open solutions**

*Vaginal dryness goes undiscussed, and quality of life is unnecessarily surrendered.*

> *A 54-year-old patient arrives after three years of vaginal discomfort. "Why didn't you come sooner?" "I was embarrassed," she says. That sentence is shared by most women.*

Postmenopausal vaginal health problems affect more than half of women. Yet until medicine gave it a formal name — "Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause," a relatively new term — it was endured in silence and shame. In quality clinical practice, that silence is not acceptable; the problem is real, and the solutions are effective.

**GSM — new term, old problem**

Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM) was formally defined in 2014, evolving from the older term "vulvovaginal atrophy" — because the new term captures all the changes involving both the vulvovaginal area and the urinary tract.

With declining estrogen: vaginal tissue thins, vaginal pH rises (shifting from acidic), lubrication decreases, pelvic floor muscles may weaken, and urethral mucosa thins.

**Symptoms**

Dryness — discomfort, itching, and burning throughout the day.

Pain during intercourse (dyspareunia). When prolonged, women begin to avoid intercourse; the partner relationship is affected.

Increased urinary tract infections — instead of one to two per year, four to five. Recurrent UTIs are common in the postmenopausal period.

Urinary incontinence — particularly stress-type (with coughing or sneezing) increases.

Changes in vaginal discharge, decreased libido — natural consequences of physical discomfort.

**Solutions — local estrogen**

The most effective treatment for GSM is local vaginal estrogen therapy. This is different from systemic HRT — it acts only on the vaginal tissue and very little enters the bloodstream. For this reason, it can often be used safely even in women with a history of breast cancer (in consultation with their oncologist).

Formulations: vaginal estrogen cream (once daily for 2–3 weeks, then a maintenance schedule of 2–3 nights per week), vaginal estrogen tablet (a small insertable tablet), vaginal estrogen ring (a silicone ring replaced every 3 months, providing continuous low-dose delivery).

Results are seen within 3–6 months. Treatment is continued indefinitely; symptoms return if it is stopped. Side effects are minimal.

**Lubricants and moisturisers**

Vaginal moisturiser for regular use — water-based, containing hyaluronic acid. Not for intercourse but for daily comfort. Two to three times per week.

Lubricant during intercourse — water-based, silicone-based, or oil-based options exist. Water-based is the most common.

Moisturiser and lubricant are not interchangeable; they are used together. Combined use with local estrogen creates a synergistic effect.

**Laser treatment**

Vaginal laser (MonaLisa Touch, Femilift, and similar devices) is used to regenerate tissue. Three to four sessions, in-office, painless.

Evidence level is moderate — some studies positive, some showing limited effect. The FDA issued a caution regarding the marketing of these devices in 2018; the evidence is not yet fully established.

Practical position: may be tried in women who do not want to use local estrogen or who have had an insufficient response to it. Not a "first choice"; local estrogen comes first. Cost: approximately 5,000–10,000 TL per session.

**Breaking the silence**

The medical side of GSM is resolvable; the cultural side needs to be spoken about. Most women live with these symptoms for years because the feeling that "this is embarrassing to discuss" persists.

In quality clinical practice, that embarrassment is the system's responsibility, not the patient's. A good gynecologist should ask about vaginal health in postmenopausal women.

If your doctor does not ask, you open the topic. "I would like to talk about something..." The response you will usually receive — "Many women ask the same thing."

> ***Dr. note —** Vaginal health in the postmenopausal period is a topic that must be spoken about — not a source of shame, but a solvable problem. Local estrogen can change your life within three months; all it takes is starting.*

**4.3**

**Empty nest syndrome — loss or an opening door**

*As your children leave, you also earn the right to your own departure.*

> *A 57-year-old patient — last child has left for university. "The house is so quiet." "Quiet can be a gift," I said. It was not the answer she expected.*

Children leaving home is one of the significant transitions in a woman's life. It is the period in which an identity defined for years primarily through "active mothering" is renegotiated. For most women this transition holds some grief, then some disorientation, and ultimately a redefinition.

In this article we will discuss the empty nest experience, the identity shift, reconnecting as a couple, and opening new spaces. Because the departure of children is not an ending — it is another beginning.

**Empty nest syndrome — real and normal**

When children leave home, a significant number of women experience the empty nest feeling. Symptoms: sadness, questioning the meaning of life, a sense of lost identity, disruption to daily routine, growing distance in the marriage.

These feelings are normal. When the daily routine has been child-centred for years — morning wake-ups, making breakfast, school runs, evening meals, bedtime routines — the absence of that routine is felt immediately.

However, "syndrome" can be a misleading word. It is not a disease; it is a transition period. For most women a new equilibrium forms within six to twelve months. If depressive symptoms persist beyond that, professional support is indicated.

**Identity shift**

The core question of the empty nest transition is the identity question. "Who am I beyond being a mother?" This question feels shameful because it can evoke the feeling of "being a bad mother." Yet motherhood is part of who you are — not the whole of it.

When the mother identity has been dominant across years, other identities — woman, spouse, professional, friend, self — may have receded. Now these identities return to the light; but having been suppressed for a long time, the questions emerge: which identities are still alive, and which have withered?

This questioning is unsettling but can become a gift. Most women in their late fifties to early sixties say they "found themselves again." New hobbies, new friendships, new career directions are common at this age.

**Reconnecting as a couple**

Children leaving creates two possible paths for a couple.

First path: drifting apart as the shared project ends. If the marriage was defined across years primarily as "raising children together," when the children leave there is no "shared project" remaining. At that point, the couple either consciously creates a new shared project or gradually drifts apart.

Second path: rediscovery. After years of parallel life, the couple re-encounters each other anew. Topics, dreams, and activities that could not be given time while children were at home now come back to the table. These couples generally experience their fifties and sixties as the richest period of the relationship.

Which path you take depends on conscious decisions. Starting the conversation with your partner before the children leave — "what will we do afterward?" — provides preparation. A shared travel plan, a new activity, a home renovation project — small beginnings are enough.

**New hobbies, volunteering, career reinvention**

The empty nest period is a natural time for new pursuits. Practical suggestions:

Hobby — things deferred for years because "there's no time" are now possible. Book club, music lessons, cooking class, gardening, dance, language learning. Learning something new has proven neuroprotective effects for the brain.

Volunteering — a powerful path to community connection and creating meaning. Women's shelters, elder care centres, cancer charities, educational NGOs — areas where meaningful contribution is possible.

Career reinvention — some women reinvent their careers during the empty nest period. Transitioning to a new field, starting a business, consultancy. "It's too late" does not apply; knowledge and experience grow as capital with the years.

Part-time study — returning to university, new certification programmes, online courses. An increasingly common choice in the era of premium aging.

**The transformation of the mother–adult child relationship**

As children grow, the relationship between mother and adult child also transforms. When this transformation is not made consciously, it becomes a source of conflict.

When a child becomes an adult, maternal intervention should step back. Advice is given when asked, not when uninvited. Lessons are observed but the child has the right to make their own mistakes.

This boundary can be difficult, because the years-long habit of the "worried mother" role is not easily set aside. But when it is not set aside, the adult child begins to withdraw — calls less often, maintains more distance. To the mother, this feels like "my child has forgotten me"; the real message is that the style of mothering has not changed.

New role: friend-like, but with a slight remove — still a mother. Advice is given, the child's own choices are respected, boundaries are drawn ("I am not getting involved in this"), open communication is maintained.

> ***Dr. note —** The nest may be physically empty; your inner world can remain full. Your children's departure shows not that mothering has ended, but that it has transformed. Filling the new chapter with new possibilities — unsettling, but entirely doable.*

**4.4**

**Hair and menopause — as one opens, another closes**

*Hair loss in the fifties is often a sign of menopause — not a cosmetic problem.*

> *A 53-year-old patient: "My hair is falling out." Bloodwork, ferritin, thyroid — all normal. "It's hormonal," I said. I am not a hair specialist, but I know the hormonal architecture.*

Hair change during menopause affects the majority of women but is rarely discussed. Because hair loss is not seen as a medical matter — it is treated as a cosmetic problem. Yet the underlying mechanism is entirely medical: the effect of hormonal transformation on the skin's appendages.

In this article we will discuss the causes of menopausal hair change, treatment options, and the path of natural acceptance.

**Menopause + relative androgen dominance**

After menopause, estrogen falls; androgen levels (testosterone and its derivatives) do not actually rise, but become relatively dominant. This "androgen dominance" sends a different signal to the hair follicles.

Estrogen normally extends the hair growth cycle, supporting follicles to remain active for longer. When it falls, the hair cycle shortens.

Androgens cause miniaturisation (shrinking) of the hair follicle and ultimately inactivation. This process progresses cumulatively over years.

**Female-pattern androgenetic alopecia in the 50s**

Male-pattern baldness (androgenetic alopecia) is well recognised in men — the frontal hairline recedes, the crown opens. In women the picture is different.

Female androgenetic alopecia typically presents as: loss of hair density at the crown, widening of the parting, thinning of individual hair strands. The frontal hairline is usually preserved.

The process generally advances slowly. Hair density decreases over years. In some women significant baldness develops; in others only density loss remains.

**Treatment options**

Topical minoxidil solution — the best-evidenced treatment. 2% or 5% concentration. Applied to the scalp twice daily. Results begin to appear within 3–6 months. The effect is lost if treatment is stopped.

Oral spironolactone — anti-androgenic effect. Reduces androgen signalling at the hair follicle. Requires a prescription; blood pressure and potassium are monitored.

PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma) treatment — plasma separated from the patient's own blood is injected into the scalp. Three to four sessions, once a month. Effective in some women, not in others. Cost approximately 5,000–15,000 TL per session.

Mesotherapy — a mixture of vitamins, minerals, and peptides applied to the scalp. Valuable as a supportive treatment.

Hair transplant — less common in women than in men. May be considered in advanced cases.

**Changed hair care routine**

A menopausal hair care routine is different. The rules for younger hair do not apply at this stage.

Shampoo — not a clarifying formula but one that contains gentle moisturising agents. An excessively stripping shampoo makes menopausal hair even drier.

Conditioner — increase quantity and frequency. A hair mask once or twice a week.

Reduce heat styling — hair dryer, curling iron, straightener. When unavoidable, use a heat protectant spray.

Choice of hairdresser — some hairdressers think only of aesthetics and neglect hair health. Choosing one who prioritises hair health matters.

**Embracing natural grey**

Separate from hair loss: the greying of hair. This is genetically determined; menopause does not cause it, but it accelerates at this life stage.

Vogue Italia, T Magazine, and other premium publications have featured grey hair as an editorial trend for years. Maye Musk, Andie MacDowell, Diane Keaton — appearing as premium icons of aging, grey-haired women.

In Turkey, acceptance of grey hair has not yet fully matured; the pressure to "not look old" makes dyeing feel obligatory. In the premium world, the choice is now yours — neither dyeing nor not dyeing is the "right" answer. As long as the decision is conscious, both are dignified.

Grey hair care is its own topic. A purple-tinted shampoo controls yellowish tones; regular toning keeps grey looking "brilliant grey"; hair texture may change and become drier.

> ***Dr. note —** Panic over hair loss generally does more damage than the loss itself — a calm approach is important. First: bloodwork (ferritin, thyroid, hormone panel), then a dermatologist or gynecologist assessment. When treatment begins, patience is essential; results are measured in months to years, not weeks.*

**4.5**

**Rediscovering sexuality — menopause's least-discussed side**

*Sexual life does not end at menopause — it can begin again.*

> *A 58-year-old patient: "My sexual life has improved." I rarely hear that sentence. The reason? GSM treatment + a secure relationship + mental ease. When all three come together, a second spring is real.*

Postmenopausal sexual life is a subject that even the medical world does not discuss enough. The prevailing assumption is that sexuality naturally ends with age. This assumption is incorrect and harmful; it pushes women to unnecessarily surrender their sexual health.

In this article we will discuss the realities of postmenopausal sexuality, the new partnership period, and both the physical and psychological factors involved.

**Postmenopausal sexuality — dismantling the assumption of loss**

The data says this: more than half of women between 50 and 69 are sexually active. Over 70, this proportion falls but a meaningful number remain active. The complete "ending" of sexuality is a widespread myth, not a biological reality.

The postmenopausal woman has three advantages from a sexual perspective: no fear of pregnancy, no periods, and a much better knowledge of herself. These are three significant gains; for many women, postmenopausal sexuality is more relaxed and self-directed than it was in their thirties.

Disadvantages: physical discomfort related to GSM, decreased libido, changes in body image. All of these are manageable.

**GSM treatment as the foundation for sexual life**

To preserve sexual life after menopause, GSM treatment is the essential foundation. Detailed in the previous article: local estrogen, lubricant, moisturiser, laser if needed.

If pain during intercourse is present, resolving the pain comes first. When painful intercourse is endured for years, the woman's sexual desire mechanism is disrupted — the body registers intercourse as a threat, and libido is suppressed.

Local estrogen + effective lubricant + a patient partner = sexual life becoming possible again. A meaningful change is seen within 3–6 months.

**Libido — psychological factors**

Sexual desire is not only hormonal; it may be as much — if not more — psychological. Areas worth addressing for postmenopausal libido:

Body image — aging, weight changes, skin changes, bodily acceptance. "Does my partner still find me attractive?" is a question with a real effect on sexuality.

Self-confidence — the personal confidence built over years enriches sexuality. After menopause, women generally know themselves better and can express what they want more freely.

Relationship quality — sexual desire is nourished by emotional connection. If you communicate well with your partner and feel closeness, sexual life is nourished too. The reverse is equally true.

Stress and fatigue — destroys libido at any age. After menopause, children are generally grown, career pace is reduced, a less stressful period — this creates conditions for sexuality.

**A new partner — after loss or divorce**

Some women meet a new partner after menopause — after the loss of a spouse, after divorce, or as a conscious new beginning.

This new partnership period calls for practical awareness.

STI screening — regardless of age, screening before sexual activity with a new partner is sensible. HIV, HPV, chlamydia, gonorrhoea, herpes — transmitted without reference to age. The old assumption "older women don't get STIs" is medically incorrect.

Protection — no pregnancy risk, but STI risk remains. Condom use with a new partner in the initial period is sensible.

Sexual health conversation — open communication with the new partner. Vaginal dryness, GSM, libido status — information sharing, not shame.

**Drug interactions**

In women over 50, medication use increases — heart, blood pressure, blood sugar, depression. Some of these medications directly affect libido.

Beta-blockers — can reduce libido.

SSRI antidepressants — libido reduction is a common side effect.

Diuretics — can worsen vaginal dryness.

Urinary incontinence medications — can worsen vaginal dryness.

If you are experiencing sexual difficulty and a new medication has recently been started, speak with your doctor. In most cases, an alternative medication can be found.

> ***Dr. note —** Sexual health has not died — age made it invisible, but making it visible is our right. Sexually active women exist at 60, 70, and 80; the woman who invests in her health preserves sexual life as part of quality of life even then.*

**4.6**

**The heart after 50 — the main concern**

*As estrogen protection wanes, women's cardiac risk converges with men's.*

> *A 56-year-old patient: "My father had a heart attack at 60. Will I?" Risk is lifestyle, not just lineage — most of the time.*

Heart disease is the leading cause of death in women — killing more women than breast cancer, gynecological cancers, and all other diseases combined. Yet in media and health communication, breast cancer dominates; heart disease is perceived as "a man's disease." This misconception is dangerous.

In this article we will discuss the specific characteristics of heart disease in women, the rising risk after menopause, and protective strategies.

**Estrogen's cardioprotective effect**

Estrogen is a natural cardioprotective agent for women. Its effects are multifaceted.

It improves cholesterol profile — keeps HDL ("good cholesterol") high and LDL ("bad cholesterol") low.

It keeps vessel walls flexible — preventing endothelial dysfunction.

It balances blood pressure — a positive effect on vascular reactivity.

Anti-inflammatory effect — reduces vascular inflammation.

For this reason, premenopausal women carry significantly lower cardiovascular risk than men of the same age. After menopause, this gap closes; by age 65, cardiovascular disease rates in women and men converge.

**Cardiac symptoms in women — different from men**

The classic heart attack presentation — chest pain, pain radiating to the left arm, profuse sweating — is the male pattern. Women are less likely to present with this typical picture.

Cardiac symptoms typical in women: back pain, jaw pain, nausea, unexpected fatigue, shortness of breath, dizziness. These symptoms can be interpreted as "perhaps I have flu" or "perhaps it's menopause."

The result: delayed diagnosis and delayed hospital presentation in women experiencing a heart attack, leading to inadequate treatment. This is one of the primary reasons why cardiac mortality in women is as high as in men.

In quality clinical practice, this awareness matters. If a woman is experiencing unusual symptoms, both doctor and patient should think of the heart. A cardiology assessment may take priority over a diagnosis of "anxiety."

**Risk factors**

Modifiable risk factors:

Blood pressure — ideal below 120/80; hypertension above 130/80. Annual measurement; monitoring if elevated.

Lipid profile — total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides. Annual measurement from age 40+.

Blood glucose — fasting glucose, HbA1c. Diabetes or pre-diabetes should be identified early.

Abdominal fat — waist circumference above 88 cm is a risk signal for women.

Smoking — the strongest modifiable risk factor. Years are gained by stopping.

Physical inactivity — 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week is the minimum target.

Nutrition — the Mediterranean pattern offers meaningful protection.

Stress — chronic stress is an independent risk factor.

Sleep — irregular or insufficient sleep increases risk.

Non-modifiable factors: age, family history, postmenopausal status.

**Nutrition + movement + sleep = cardiac protection**

The Mediterranean dietary pattern is the most evidence-supported protective strategy. Abundant vegetables and fruit, whole grains, olive oil, fish (two to three times per week), legumes, limited red meat, minimal processed food.

Movement — aerobic exercise (walking, swimming, cycling) for 150 minutes per week + strength training two to three times per week. This combination is effective.

Sleep — seven to nine hours per night. Insufficient sleep increases cardiac risk; obstructive sleep apnoea is an underrecognised risk in women over 50.

Stress management — yoga, meditation, walks in nature, social connection. "Yoga protects the heart" is not a metaphor; it has been demonstrated in randomised trials.

**HRT and the heart**

HRT's effect on the heart depends on age and timing.

HRT started before age 60, during perimenopause or early postmenopause — within the framework of the "timing hypothesis" — may be cardioprotective. It has a positive effect on younger vascular tissue.

HRT started after age 60 — after years of estrogen absence — may be neutral or mildly harmful from a cardiac risk standpoint. At this age, HRT is not started solely for cardiac protection.

This timing difference was the core reason for the misinterpretation of the 2002 WHI study. The study was conducted in women aged 60+; the "timing hypothesis" was not yet established at that time.

Practical message: the right time for HRT is before or immediately after menopause. Not starting late — talking early is what matters.

> ***Dr. note —** Protecting the female heart begins at fifty, not at sixty. The check-up forgotten after the mammogram — the cardiology assessment. Blood pressure, cholesterol, blood glucose: annually. The symptoms are different; "this could be my heart" is a thought that saves lives.*
